When embarking on a coding project, it is key that decision-making processes are free from bias. Unfair assessment of data can lead to inaccurate results or conclusions and impede the success of the team's efforts in completing their task efficiently. Unfortunately, biases can easily creep into our thought processes if we are not aware of how they work and how to avoid them, even though some may be subconsciously present without us noticing at all. Below, I will explain five different types of potential cognitive biases that teams should watch out for when making important decisions related to their ISPs: Confirmation Bias, Groupthink, Attribution Error, Status Quo Bias, and In-Group Bias.

Confirmation bias is a common tendency for people to only consider information that confirms their prior beliefs while disregarding evidence that contradicts those views; this often leads individuals (or groups) down an illogical path, as opposed to staying open-minded about alternative possibilities in solving complex problems within a given time frame. For example, let's imagine you're working with your development team to brainstorm ideas for fixing a bug within an existing codebase, but every idea proposed by someone who disagrees with yours gets quickly discounted because "it won't work". This could become especially problematic if confirmation bias continues unchecked, preventing group members from seeing other options available, leading them further away from finding a successful solution and shortening the timeline allocated to complete the goal set forth initially.

Groupthink occurs whenever there is pressure placed on individual members not to speak up against majority opinion due to fear of repercussions such as being ostracized or dismissed entirely from discussion. Unfortunately, what usually ends up happening is that the collective thinking process becomes limited in scope, and the original intention of creating a tunnel vision so everyone focuses solely on a particular aspect of the project is lost. Instead of addressing each problem objectively and openly discussing possible solutions together until the best answer is found collaboratively as a unit, people go off independently to pursue their own interests regardless of the outcome others might face afterwards.

Attribution error occurs when attributing a cause to a certain event even though none exists. This means assigning the wrong person blame for a situation based on personal judgments or judgement calls made incorrectly (Healy). It is attributed to another factor where no reasonable connection between the two exists whatsoever, such as unfair criticism of a fellow coworker for writing poor quality code and then blaming their inexperience level with the tech language used to address the issue itself, thus diverting attention onto something else to distract onlookers from the reality that the more likely culprit had nothing to do with the actual circumstances surrounding the case (Healy). That is why it is essential to always take extra care in formulating an accurate narrative before jumping to a definitive conclusion that assigns responsibility to a specific source too hastily, just to satisfy an immediate need to point the finger at a scapegoat or handy target for supposed wrongdoing committed elsewhere.

Status quo bias refers to the prevailing behavior exhibited by most people to maintain the current state of affairs despite whatever changes the external environment presents over a period of time. Whether these benefits the organization or detriment the end user experience does not matter as long as the status remains the same (Cherry). The general direction taken goes unchallenged, otherwise known as a "laziness" attitude since there is a lack of motivation to drive change. Anything that already works well enough now risks losing any gains achieved so far on the journey, which would ultimately result in a monumental waste of resources expended to get the venture underway and begin to start again from somewhere near zero. Experiments should follow, making sure to weigh the pros and cons wisely to help achieve the desired objectives, resulting in reaching goals quicker and easier.

Finally, in-group bias is a type of social prejudice that results from favoring the opinions and viewpoints held by members close to our circle due to physical similarities or shared behavioral characteristics (Decision Lab). This can lead group dynamics astray, as any suggestion made outside this inner bubble might be rejected, even if backed up with solid evidence, because it does not align with their preexisting line of thinking. Therefore, giving preferential treatment to ideas only originating within one's own special clique will impede progress and create an atmosphere where minority voices are completely dismissed without being seriously considered for the potential value they may bring to the chosen path.
